Two men sit perched on a heavy, white plastic road barrier at the perimeter of the Qatar Petroleum District construction site, offering a snapshot of the informal social dynamics and hierarchies among the migrant workforce. The setting is purely utilitarian: a dusty roadside against the plain hoarding of the site fence, underscoring the lack of dedicated, comfortable rest areas for the men building Doha's luxury infrastructure.​

The differences in their attire point to the rigid, color-coded class system prevalent on Gulf construction sites. The man on the left wears the standard issue gear of a general laborer or tradesman: a bright orange hard hat, a yellow high-visibility vest over a casual jacket and jeans, and scuffed work boots. In contrast, the man on the right wears a green hard hat—often designating a safety officer, supervisor, or foreman—along with a noticeably cleaner, sportier jacket and pristine blue uniform trousers. His posture is relaxed but authoritative; he sits slightly higher on the barrier, arms crossed, engaged in a phone call, suggesting a role that involves coordination rather than constant physical exertion.​​

Despite the difference in their apparent ranks, the two share a quiet, companionable moment during a break. The laborer on the left holds a cigarette and offers a slight, weary smile to the camera. Their shared rest on the concrete barrier highlights that, regardless of their specific roles or the color of their hats, all migrant workers in this system are ultimately bound by the same harsh environmental realities and restrictive kafala labor conditions that defined the era.
