A closer view of the migrant laborers resting along the pavement in West Bay reveals the individual faces and distinct uniforms behind the massive Qatar Petroleum District project. Lined up against a low concrete boundary wall, the men are clearly exhausted, their postures conveying the deep physical toll of a 10-hour shift. Several sit with their legs splayed or crossed, while others rest their heads against the wall, their eyes closed or staring blankly ahead in quiet resignation as they wait for transport back to their labor camps.​​

The workers' attire tells a story of diverse roles and harsh working conditions. Most wear the standard light blue coveralls paired with high-visibility yellow-green vests, though one man is dressed in a dark jacket, and another wears a red vest. Their hard hats, scattered around them or still worn, act as a color-coded hierarchy: yellow for general laborers and earth-moving crews, and blue for technical workers like electricians or carpenters. To shield themselves from the sun and pervasive construction dust, many have improvised face coverings, wrapping patterned scarves tightly around their heads beneath their helmets or pulling up the hoods of their coveralls.​​

The backdrop further emphasizes the juxtaposition between the laborers and the environment they are building. Just beyond the green metal fencing, the sleek, modern architecture of Doha's West Bay rises—including the massive Ezdan Hotel complex and the reflective glass facades of neighboring high-rises. The laborers, covered in dust and sitting on the hard paving stones with no dedicated rest area, form the invisible, human foundation of this rapidly modernizing, ultra-wealthy skyline.
